Dr. Fedrick Banting

The man who saved a billion lives

Here's a time line of Dr. Banting's life:


  • 1891 - Frederick Grant Banting was born on the family farm in Alliston, Ontario, Canada
  • 1910 - Entered Victoria College of the University of Toronto to pursue a career in Methodist Ministry but left before the end of the first year
  • 1912 - Entered University of Toronto Faculty of Medicine 1T7, with a specialty in surgery
  • 1914 - Fred’s farm neighbour and friend with diabetes, Jennie Victoria Jorden, died at age 14. Fred was a pallbearer at her funeral
  • 1914 - Upon declaration of war, Banting attempted to enlist in the Canadian Armed Forces but was rejected due to his poor eyesight
  • 1914 - Banting attempted, for a second time, to enlist in the Canadian Armed Forces but was rejected again due to his poor eyesight
  • 1915 - Enrolled in the Canadian Army Medical Service as a Private
  • 1916 - Graduated with an M.B., University of Toronto; along with others in an accelerated class due to the Great War
  • 1917 - Lieutenant Banting sailed to Britain
  • 1917 - Served at Granville Canadian Special Hospital, under Lieutenant-Colonel Starr in Ramsgate, Kent, across the Channel from the French Battlefields
  • 1918 - Posted as a Medical Officer to the 44th Battalion, 4th Canadian Division in Arras
  • 1918 - Awarded Military Cross for Bravery on the Front
  • 1919 - Returned to Canada and was posted to Christie Street Hospital for Veterans in Toronto for six months; Released from the Army
  • 1919 - Intern at the Hospital for Sick Children, Toronto
  • 1920 - Resident Surgeon at the Hospital for Sick Children, Toronto. Began his practice in Medicine in London, Ontario, where he lived for ten months.
  • 1920 - Part-time teaching assistant in Physiology at the University of Western Ontario at London, Ontario. Awoke with “eureka” idea of how to isolate insulin after reading the article ‘The Relation of the Islets of Langerhans to Diabetes with Special Reference to Cases of Pancreatic Lithiasis’ by Moses Barron, M.D. Met with John James Rickard Macleod (1876-1935) at the University of Toronto about his idea; Macleod offered lab space, an assistant, and dogs.
  • 1921 - Began Isletin research at the University of Toronto, assisted by Charles Herbert Best (1899-1978). Banting and Best successfully isolated the hormone Isletin (later named Insulin). First positive results from injection of Isletin on a Terrier dog Number 410 while Macleod was in Scotland. Velyien Henderson, Head of Pharmacology Department, offered Banting a position as a demonstrator, allowing for Banting’s Insulin work to continue. Banting and Best presented their findings before the Physiological Journal Club of the University of Toronto. Began Longevity Test on dog Number 33. Banting and Best injected each other with Insulin. Delivered first public lecture entitled ‘The Beneficial Influences of Certain Pancreatic Extracts on Pancreatic Diabetes’ before an audience of prominent scientists and clinicians at Yale University, including F.M. Allen, E.P Joslin, and G.H.A. Clowes.
  • 1922 - With the help of Dr. Joseph Gilchrist, and the support of the Department of Soldier’s Civil Re-establishment, Banting established a Diabetic Clinic at the Christie Street Hospital for Veterans and opened his own practice on 160 Bloor Street West where he treated private patients.
  • 1923 - The Ontario Legislative Assembly passed the Banting and Best Medical Research Act that established the Banting and Best Chair of Medical Research, for which Banting was appointed the first director and was funded with an annual grant of $10,000. The Dominion Government awarded Banting a lifetime annuity of $7500. Banting opened the Canadian National Exhibition where he spoke to a crowd of 76500 people. Banting became the first Canadian to win a Nobel Prize in Medicine which was conferred jointly on Banting and Macleod.
  • 1927 - Began a painting expedition to the Arctic with A.Y. Jackson.
  • 1930 - University of Toronto dedicated a building to Banting named The Banting Institute.
  • 1934 - Banting was Knighted by King George V.
  • 1938 - Invited to become the first chairman for the Associate Committee on Medical Research of the National Research Council.
  • 1939 - Banting was among the first to offer his services to his Country and rejoined the Royal Canadian Army Medical Corps for the Second World War.
  • 1941 - As part of his war service, Banting was en route to England the night when his plane, a Lockheed Hudson Bomber, crashed on the east coast of Newfoundland. Shortly after the takeoff, the plane developed mechanical problems and the pilot, J.C. Mackey, attempted to bring the plane down near Musgrave Harbour, but hit a tree on landing. Both the operator and the navigator died on impact and Banting was fatally injured. On February 21st, Banting died as a result of his injuries, leaving one son, William, and his second wife, Lady Henrietta Ball Banting. March 4th, Banting is buried at Mount Pleasant Cemetery in Toronto, Ontario.

A famous qoute by Sir Frederick Grant Banting
